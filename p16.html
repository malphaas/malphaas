Cosines and sines of all constructible angles of integer degrees

As we know from Gauss, only angles corresponding to equal divisions of a circle into powers of two times a product of non-repeating Fermat primes are the only ones that are constructible classically by straightedge and compass. Which is to say 2*pi/n, where n = 2^k * F0^p1 * F1^p2 * .... (at present only F0,F1,F2,F3,F4 are known to be prime, where F_i = 2**2**i + 1) with the condition that pi is either 0 or 1 and k can be any non-negative integer. Since we are only concerned with angles having integer degree values we are further uninterested in angles like 2pi/17 which are fractional values in degrees. Obviously we have no need to compute angles beyond 90 degree since they are derived from values of cosines and sines in [0,pi/2]. Henceforth we shall only use degrees instead of radians, and the degree symbol shall be omitted for brevity.

Construct a regular pentagon ABCDEF of side 1. Drop a perpendicular from apex A to a point T on opposide side DC. This bisects DC equally into segments of length 1/2. Also draw diagonals from apex to D and C. The internal angle of the regular pentagon is 108 degrees. AE=ED=1 thus ∠EAD = ∠EDA = 36. Similar arguments for triangle ABC. Thus, ∠DAC=108-36-36 = 36, and the half angle is 18 degrees. Computing the length of AD two different ways, we get (1/2)*(1/cos 72) = 2 cos 36, or cos 72 cos 36 = 1/4. Using the fact that cos(72 + 36 ) + cos(72-36) = 2 cos 72 cos 36 = cos 108 + cos 36 = - sin 18 + cos 36 = 1/2. Or, -x + 2*x**2 - 1 = 1/2 where x = sin 18 and using the expression cos 2x = 1 - 2 sin^2(x). Solving this equation and taking the positive root yields sin 18. Using this value we may compute sines and cosines of 18, 36, 54, 72. The schemes for the remaining angles are as follows. For cos 9 and sin 9 I use the double angle cosine formulae or cos(x/2) = sqrt( (1 + cos(x))/2 ) and sin(x/2) = sqrt ( (1 - cos(x))/2 ) to derive their values from those of the already computed values of 18. 6 degrees is computed as 36-30. 12 = 72 - 60. 24 = 144 - 120. 3 by halving the cosine of 6 again. 15 by halving 30. 21 as 45-24. 27 as 45-18. 33 as 45-12. 39 as 45-6. 42 as 90-48, and 48 itself as 120-72. All subsequent angles are obtained by subtraction from 90 degrees since we have a table of values for all angles less than or equal to 45 already.

3 degrees is the smallest integer valued angle in degrees that is constructible by straightedge and compass. Any smaller values such as 2 leads to n = 360/2 = 180 having the fermat prime 3 twice. 1 degree or 360/1 divisions suffers the same problem. You may verify that all other integer degree angles in [0,90] either have repeating fermat primes or non-fermat primes in factorization of 360/angle. A table listing of all those values as computed above follows. We can observe some beautiful symmetries in conjugation/complementation of the radical terms, and we also note that only square roots of 2, 3 and 5 occur (upto nesting).
